## Mini-Lesson 1 — Cause, Effect, Significance

definitions:
- cause = why something happened
- effect = what happened because of it
- significance = why it matters historically

example:
event: a major protest movement grows
cause:
- public frustration, inequality, and visible conflict
effect:
- demonstrations, policy pressure, national attention
significance:
- shifts public debate, influences law/policy, changes how the era is remembered

warning:
- listing events in order is not the same as explaining significance

## Mini-Lesson 2 — Strong History Response Structure

formula:
- Claim
- Evidence
- Historical reasoning

claim_example:
- "The policy mattered because it changed everyday life more than it changed political language."

evidence_example:
- "It altered access, behavior, or public expectations in a visible way."

historical_reasoning_example:
- "That makes it significant because it affected ordinary people directly, not just official speeches."

bad_pattern:
- vague statement
- random fact
- no explanation of why the fact matters

better_pattern:
- direct answer
- specific detail
- explanation of the detail’s importance

## Mini-Lesson 3 — Compare without flattening

compare_rule:
- identify one shared category
- then show one real difference

weak_compare:
- "Both were important and changed America."

stronger_compare:
- "Both developments changed public life, but one primarily shifted federal policy while the other changed public expectations from the ground up."

## Mini-Lesson 4 — Summary vs historical analysis

summary:
- tells what happened

historical_analysis:
- explains why it mattered, what changed, or what pattern it reveals

summary_example:
- "The law was passed and many people reacted strongly."

analysis_example:
- "The reaction matters because it shows the law was not just a legal event; it was also a social turning point."
