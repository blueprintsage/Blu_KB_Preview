## Mini-Lesson 1 — What is the prompt really asking?

rule:
- break the prompt into:
  - task
  - text target
  - lens
  - evidence expectation

example_prompt_1:
- "How does the speaker’s tone shape the meaning of the passage?"
breakdown:
- task: explain how
- text target: speaker’s tone
- lens: meaning
- evidence expectation: details from the passage

bad_start:
- "This passage is interesting and has a lot going on."

better_start:
- "The speaker’s bitter tone makes the passage feel less like a celebration and more like a warning."

why:
- the better start answers the prompt directly
- it gives a claim someone could prove from the text

## Mini-Lesson 2 — Claim + Evidence + Reasoning

formula:
- Claim
- Evidence
- Reasoning

claim_example:
- "The narrator’s detached tone makes the violence feel more unsettling."

evidence_example:
- "The passage describes the scene in flat, ordinary language instead of emotional language."

reasoning_example:
- "Because the narrator avoids dramatic reaction, the event feels abnormal in a colder, more disturbing way."

warning:
- evidence without reasoning is incomplete
- reasoning without evidence sounds invented

## Mini-Lesson 3 — Summary vs analysis

summary:
- tells what happened

analysis:
- explains what the detail does

summary_example:
- "The character leaves the house and walks to the river."

analysis_example:
- "The walk to the river marks a shift from safety to uncertainty, which mirrors the character’s inner conflict."

test:
- if the sentence could appear in a plot recap, it probably is not analysis yet

## Mini-Lesson 4 — Weak answer repair

common_weaknesses:
- vague claim
- too much retelling
- quote dropped in without explanation
- answer ignores half the prompt
- conclusion just repeats the first sentence

repair_moves:
- replace vague adjectives with text-rooted language
- cut retelling and keep only the evidence you need
- add a why-sentence after every piece of evidence
- restate the exact lens from the prompt
- end by showing what the evidence proves
